02 — Problem

How do you build a personalized horror experience — delivered live in a browser — that still feels like a surprise?

The Core Challenge

Create a high-impact horror experience for a specific streamer — without installation, without setup — that leverages their own identity, history, and community as the source of dread.

The experience had to function as a one-time event. A live anniversary stream. No patching after the fact. No second impressions. Reliability and emotional timing weren't quality-of-life concerns — they were the entire design constraint.

Design Goals

Instant browser access during a live stream — zero friction for a live audience.

Felt made for that specific streamer. Not generic horror — personal horror.

Used familiar streaming elements — chat, community, the streamer's own face — as instruments of unease.

A controlled psychological arc: passive viewer → direct target.

03 — Constraints

Every decision was made inside a box.

Platform

Browser-only. No installation. Smooth real-time performance despite live chat feed, audio triggers, and scene transitions. Target: sub-megabyte total size — inspired by demoscene projects like .kkrieger.

Time

Two-week hard deadline tied to a fixed live event. No post-launch iteration window. Every decision had to account for a final test happening in public, in real time.

Technical

First browser-based game. Limited prior experience with JavaScript game systems and real-time interaction design. Systems had to be learned and implemented simultaneously while building under deadline.

Team

Solo across design, development, and implementation. No handoffs, no review cycles. Every decision lived and died in the same session it was made.

Core Interaction Loop

Chat messages stream continuously. Each triggers a synchronized audio cue — TTS, mechanical sounds, analog noise. The player reads, hears, anticipates. Repetition and timing build the rhythm. The rhythm builds dread.

Player Awareness System

Player movement is tracked as a proxy for engagement. If the player stops, chat messages adapt to reflect that stillness. The system creates an illusion of being watched. The perception of awareness is the mechanism — not the reality.

Room-Based Escalation

Five rooms, each a discrete system state. Transitioning triggers a full shift across all systems simultaneously — chat tone, audio density, environmental content. Each door closes behind the player. No going back.

Sensory Systems

Sound design references 70s/80s analog technology — Speak & Spell TTS, mechanical chatter, electrical hum. PSX-inspired low-fidelity rendering. Dim, subtly pulsing light. These aren't chosen for nostalgia — they evoke machinery that is functional but unknowable.

Progression Logic

Discrete state changes, not continuous scaling. Each room is a checkpoint where systems update together. This allows pacing to be authored exactly — important for a one-time event where timing had to land.

05 — Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

Every constraint produced a decision. Most produced better outcomes than the unconstrained alternatives.

Before

Linear room layout — players could see the next space, making progression feel safe and predictable.

After

Circular layout — no forward visibility, replacing clarity with ambiguity.

Why it mattered: Knowing what's coming ahead is the opposite of horror. One layout change removed player certainty entirely. Same rooms, reordered. Atmosphere improved significantly.

Before

Fully procedural environments — no stored assets, rooms generated on the fly. Rooms became visually repetitive.

After

Behavioral escalation — identical spaces that act differently across states. Visual monotony replaced by system-driven variation.

Why it mattered: The rooms don't change — the way they respond to the player does. A technical limitation became a more coherent design direction.

Before

Full PSX-style shader implementation. Accurate visual style, significant in-browser lag.

After

Reduced shader complexity — essential visual markers only. Stable performance over visual accuracy.

Why it mattered: Lag breaks immersion. In a horror experience, it also breaks trust. Performance was non-negotiable. Visual accuracy was the variable.

06 — Iteration & Failure

What broke, and what changed because of it.

System Fragility

Small JavaScript errors — a missing bracket, a misplaced variable — caused full system failures. The experience broke constantly. This reinforced a practice of incremental changes and immediate validation rather than large rewrites. Stable states had to be tested after every change.

Layout Rework

The linear-to-circular layout shift was a full spatial redesign mid-development. It cost time. It was worth it because the original version demonstrably reduced the emotional effect — testable, not speculative.

Procedural Approach Abandoned

The demoscene-inspired constraint — no stored assets, fully generated — was abandoned when it produced visual monotony rather than efficiency. The goal was interesting constraint design, but the output was dull rooms. Shifting to behavioral variation preserved the spirit while serving the experience.

07 — Outcome

Stable. Playable. In final polish ahead of live deployment.

Core Systems Operational

Chat feed, audio triggers, player awareness, and room escalation are all implemented and functioning as designed. The scope is fixed. No new features are being added.

08 — Reflection

What the project actually taught.

On Systems

Layered systems produce effects that none of their parts could create alone. The chat feed is just text. Audio cues are just sounds. The awareness system is an illusion. Together they feel coherent and intentional.

On Constraints

Constraints don't limit design — they locate it. The sub-megabyte target, the two-week deadline, the solo workflow: each forced a decision. Most of those decisions produced better outcomes than unconstrained alternatives would have.

On Perception

The perception of awareness is the mechanism, not the reality. A system doesn't need to be truly reactive to feel reactive. Designing for what the user experiences is distinct from designing what's technically happening.

On Stability

Stability requires constant validation, not just initial success. Incremental changes, continuous testing, and treating every state as fragile until proven otherwise — this is the practice.
